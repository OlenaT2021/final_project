Common challenges for I18n and L10n

As companies expand globally, they face the dual challenges of internationalization (making products and services adaptable across cultures) and localization (adapting products to specific markets). While these processes serve different purposes, they share several fundamental challenges that can impact global success.

Challenge Description
Regulatory and compliance issues

Some regions have legal requirements for language support (for example, Quebec requires French).
Technical compatibility issues

Multiple language support often requires handling different character sets, text length, and directions (right-to-left vs left-to-right).

Formatting conventions (dates, currencies, numbers) and sorting order (collation). Every language has a set of rules for formatting conventions and sorting order, ​neglecting which can lead to significant issues affecting user experience, data integrity, and overall effectiveness in target markets.
Performance and storage

Supporting multiple languages can increase software size.

Some languages require larger character sets (for example, Unicode support for Asian languages).
Translation accuracy and context

Direct translations may not work well (for example, idioms and slang). It is also important to consider the need to comply with different legal systems or business environments, and different cultural determinants, as failure to do so can result in legal penalties and restricted market access.

Lack of context can lead to incorrect translations.

Updating and maintaining translations

When the source language changes, translations must be updated across all languages.
Testing and quality assurance

Testing needs to be done for each language, which increases complexity.

Automated tests may not catch layout breakages caused by long translations, issues related to dates, currencies, and numbers.

Translation isn’t just about converting text from one language to another; it’s about localizing the message so it resonates culturally and linguistically with the audience..

A poorly translated slogan or product name can confuse or offend, leading to massive losses. Like it happened to Pepsi's “Come alive” Campaign in China

In the 1960s, Pepsi's slogan "Come alive! You're the Pepsi generation" was translated for the Chinese market. However, the translation was interpreted as "Pepsi brings your ancestors back from the grave". This misinterpretation stemmed from a literal translation that didn't consider cultural nuances and idiomatic expressions in Chinese. The result was confusion and a negative reception among Chinese consumers, illustrating the pitfalls of inadequate localization.

So to meet these challenges, a comprehensive strategy is needed to address both the technology and cultural changes required for truly global products and services.
